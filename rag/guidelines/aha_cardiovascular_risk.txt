AHA — Cardiovascular Risk Reduction Guidelines
Source: American Heart Association (AHA)

OVERVIEW
Cardiovascular disease (CVD) remains the leading cause of death worldwide. The AHA provides evidence-based guidelines for risk assessment, prevention, and management of cardiovascular conditions. Early identification and management of risk factors, particularly in young adults, can significantly reduce lifetime cardiovascular risk.

BLOOD PRESSURE CLASSIFICATION
The AHA classifies blood pressure for adults as follows:
- Normal: Systolic < 120 mmHg AND Diastolic < 80 mmHg.
- Elevated: Systolic 120–129 mmHg AND Diastolic < 80 mmHg.
- Hypertension Stage 1: Systolic 130–139 mmHg OR Diastolic 80–89 mmHg.
- Hypertension Stage 2: Systolic ≥ 140 mmHg OR Diastolic ≥ 90 mmHg.
- Hypertensive Crisis: Systolic > 180 mmHg AND/OR Diastolic > 120 mmHg — seek immediate medical attention.

HEART RATE MONITORING
- Normal resting heart rate for adults: 60–100 BPM.
- A lower resting heart rate generally indicates better cardiovascular fitness.
- Resting heart rate above 100 BPM (tachycardia) may indicate stress, dehydration, fever, or underlying cardiac conditions.
- Resting heart rate below 60 BPM (bradycardia) is normal in trained athletes but may require evaluation in sedentary individuals.
- Students should monitor resting heart rate regularly; persistent elevation may signal chronic stress or cardiovascular risk.

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS FOR CVD PREVENTION
The AHA recommends Life's Essential 8 for optimal cardiovascular health:
1. Eat a healthy diet — emphasise fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats.
2. Be physically active — at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity exercise per week.
3. Avoid tobacco — do not smoke, vape, or use tobacco products.
4. Get enough sleep — 7–9 hours per night for adults aged 18–25.
5. Manage weight — maintain a healthy BMI (18.5–24.9).
6. Control cholesterol — total cholesterol below 200 mg/dL is desirable.
7. Manage blood sugar — fasting glucose below 100 mg/dL is normal.
8. Manage blood pressure — target below 120/80 mmHg.

STRESS AND CARDIOVASCULAR HEALTH
Chronic psychological stress is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease:
- Stress activates the sympathetic nervous system, releasing cortisol and adrenaline.
- Prolonged elevation increases heart rate, blood pressure, and inflammatory markers.
- Chronic stress is associated with unhealthy coping behaviours (poor diet, sedentary lifestyle, substance use).
- Effective stress management techniques include mindfulness meditation, regular physical activity, social support, and adequate sleep.

WHEN TO SEEK MEDICAL EVALUATION
Young adults should consult a healthcare provider if they experience:
- Persistent resting heart rate above 100 BPM or below 50 BPM (if not an athlete).
- Blood pressure consistently above 130/80 mmHg.
- Chest pain, palpitations, or shortness of breath during routine activities.
- Family history of premature heart disease (before age 55 in males, 65 in females).
