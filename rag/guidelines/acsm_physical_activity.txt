ACSM — Physical Activity Guidelines for College-Age Adults
Source: American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM)

OVERVIEW
Physical activity is one of the most effective interventions for maintaining physical and mental health. For college students, regular exercise improves cardiovascular fitness, reduces stress, enhances cognitive function, and supports academic performance. Despite these benefits, surveys indicate that over 50% of college students do not meet minimum physical activity recommendations.

RECOMMENDED ACTIVITY LEVELS
The ACSM classifies physical activity levels as:
- Low (Sedentary): Less than 75 minutes of physical activity per week. Predominantly sitting for work, study, and leisure. Associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease, obesity, and mental health disorders.
- Moderate: 150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week (e.g., brisk walking, recreational cycling, swimming). Meets minimum health recommendations.
- High (Active): More than 300 minutes per week or 150+ minutes of vigorous activity (e.g., running, competitive sports, HIIT). Associated with optimal health outcomes and reduced all-cause mortality.

TYPES OF RECOMMENDED EXERCISE
1. Aerobic exercise — improves cardiovascular endurance and reduces stress hormones. Examples: brisk walking, jogging, cycling, swimming, dancing. Recommended: 150–300 minutes/week at moderate intensity.
2. Resistance training — builds muscle strength and bone density. Examples: bodyweight exercises, weight training, resistance bands. Recommended: 2–3 sessions per week targeting all major muscle groups.
3. Flexibility and balance — reduces injury risk and improves posture. Examples: stretching, yoga, tai chi. Recommended: stretching after each exercise session; yoga/tai chi 1–2 times per week.
4. Neuromotor exercise — enhances coordination and proprioception. Examples: balance training, agility drills. Recommended: 2–3 times per week for older adults; beneficial for all ages.

PHYSICAL INACTIVITY RISKS
Insufficient physical activity in college students is associated with:
- Elevated resting heart rate and blood pressure.
- Weight gain and increased BMI.
- Higher levels of anxiety, depression, and perceived stress.
- Reduced sleep quality and increased insomnia.
- Lower academic performance and cognitive function.
- Increased risk of developing chronic diseases (type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease) later in life.

STRATEGIES FOR INCREASING ACTIVITY
- Active commuting: Walk or cycle to campus instead of driving.
- Study breaks: Take 5–10 minute movement breaks every hour during study sessions.
- Recreational sports: Join intramural sports teams or fitness clubs.
- Structured exercise: Schedule exercise sessions like academic commitments.
- Reduce sedentary time: Use standing desks, take stairs instead of elevators, walk during phone calls.
- Social exercise: Exercise with friends to increase adherence and enjoyment.

EXERCISE AND MENTAL HEALTH
Regular physical activity has robust evidence for mental health benefits:
- Reduces symptoms of depression and anxiety comparable to pharmacological interventions.
- Improves self-esteem, body image, and overall quality of life.
- Enhances sleep quality when performed regularly (not close to bedtime).
- Provides a healthy coping mechanism for academic and social stress.
- Releases endorphins, serotonin, and dopamine — improving mood and cognitive function.
