CDC — Sleep and Sleep Disorders: Guidelines for Young Adults and College Students
Source: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)

OVERVIEW
Adequate sleep is essential for physical health, mental well-being, cognitive performance, and academic success. Sleep deprivation is increasingly prevalent among college students, with studies showing that over 60% of students report poor sleep quality.

RECOMMENDED SLEEP DURATION
The CDC recommends the following sleep durations by age:
- Adults aged 18–25 (young adults): 7–9 hours per night.
- Adults aged 26–64: 7–9 hours per night.
- Sleeping fewer than 6 hours per night is classified as short sleep and is associated with increased health risks.

CONSEQUENCES OF POOR SLEEP
Chronic sleep deprivation in students is linked to:
- Impaired cognitive function: reduced attention span, memory consolidation, and problem-solving ability.
- Increased risk of cardiovascular disease: elevated resting heart rate, higher blood pressure, and increased stress hormones.
- Mental health deterioration: heightened anxiety, irritability, depressive symptoms, and emotional dysregulation.
- Weakened immune function: increased susceptibility to infections.
- Weight gain: disrupted appetite-regulating hormones (leptin and ghrelin).
- Reduced academic performance: lower GPA, increased absenteeism, and decreased class participation.

SLEEP HYGIENE RECOMMENDATIONS
To improve sleep quality, the CDC recommends:
- Maintain a consistent sleep schedule — go to bed and wake up at the same time daily, including weekends.
- Create a restful environment — keep the bedroom dark, quiet, and cool (18–21°C).
- Limit screen time — avoid electronic devices at least 30 minutes before bedtime.
- Avoid stimulants — limit caffeine intake after 2 PM and avoid nicotine before sleep.
- Exercise regularly — physical activity promotes better sleep, but avoid vigorous exercise within 2 hours of bedtime.
- Manage stress — practise relaxation techniques such as deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, or meditation before bed.
- Limit naps — if napping, keep it under 30 minutes and before 3 PM.
- Avoid alcohol before sleep — while it may induce drowsiness, alcohol disrupts sleep architecture and reduces REM sleep.

WHEN TO SEEK PROFESSIONAL HELP
Students should consult a healthcare provider if they:
- Consistently require more than 30 minutes to fall asleep.
- Wake frequently during the night and struggle to return to sleep.
- Experience excessive daytime sleepiness affecting daily functioning.
- Snore loudly or exhibit signs of sleep apnoea (witnessed breathing pauses).
- Rely on sleep medications for more than a few consecutive nights.
