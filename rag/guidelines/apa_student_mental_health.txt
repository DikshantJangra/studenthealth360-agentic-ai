APA — Student Mental Health and Academic Burnout Guidelines
Source: American Psychological Association (APA)

OVERVIEW
The transition to higher education presents unique psychological challenges. Academic pressure, social adjustment, financial stress, and identity development can converge to create significant mental health burden. The APA estimates that over 60% of college students experience overwhelming anxiety at some point during their studies, and approximately 40% report feeling so depressed that it is difficult to function.

UNDERSTANDING ACADEMIC BURNOUT
Burnout is a state of chronic physical and emotional exhaustion resulting from prolonged and excessive stress. In students, burnout manifests as:
- Emotional exhaustion — feeling drained, overwhelmed, and unable to cope with academic demands.
- Cynicism and detachment — loss of motivation, interest in studies, and social withdrawal.
- Reduced academic efficacy — declining grades, missed deadlines, and impaired concentration.

RISK FACTORS FOR STUDENT BURNOUT
Key risk factors identified in research include:
- Excessive workload: Study hours exceeding 45–50 hours per week (including classes, homework, and projects) significantly increase burnout risk.
- Poor sleep quality: Sleep deprivation compounds stress and reduces cognitive resilience.
- High stress levels: Both self-reported and physiological stress indicators above the 75th percentile are associated with burnout.
- Low physical activity: Sedentary students report higher levels of emotional exhaustion.
- Negative mood states: Persistent stressed or unhappy mood lasting more than 2 weeks may indicate clinical depression.
- Social isolation: Lack of peer support networks amplifies stress impact.
- Perfectionism: Unrealistic academic expectations and fear of failure drive chronic stress.

MOOD AND PSYCHOLOGICAL ASSESSMENT
Student mood states can be broadly categorised for screening purposes:
- Happy/Positive: Student reports general satisfaction, engagement, and emotional stability. Associated with lower health risk and better academic outcomes.
- Neutral: Student reports neither positive nor negative emotional state. May indicate emotional numbness or early disengagement — warrants monitoring.
- Stressed/Negative: Student reports persistent worry, irritability, sadness, or overwhelm. Strongly associated with elevated health risk and academic decline. Requires active intervention.

EVIDENCE-BASED INTERVENTIONS
1. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) — structured therapeutic approach addressing maladaptive thought patterns. Highly effective for anxiety, depression, and academic stress.
2. Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) — 8-week programme combining meditation, body awareness, and yoga. Reduces cortisol, anxiety, and rumination.
3. Academic coaching — structured support for time management, study skills, and goal-setting. Addresses root causes of academic stress.
4. Peer support programmes — trained student mentors provide emotional support and practical guidance. Reduces isolation and normalises help-seeking.
5. Physical activity prescription — regular exercise as an adjunct to psychological intervention. Demonstrated efficacy comparable to antidepressant medication for mild-moderate depression.
6. Sleep intervention — CBT for insomnia (CBT-I) addresses sleep disturbances that exacerbate mental health symptoms.

PROTECTIVE FACTORS
Factors associated with resilience and lower health risk:
- Strong social connections and peer support networks.
- Regular physical activity (minimum 150 minutes/week).
- Good sleep quality (7–9 hours, consistent schedule).
- Balanced academic workload (under 45 hours/week total).
- Active coping strategies (problem-solving, help-seeking, positive reframing).
- Engagement in extracurricular activities and hobbies.

WHEN TO ESCALATE
Immediate referral to mental health services is indicated when a student:
- Expresses suicidal ideation or self-harm intent.
- Exhibits sudden and dramatic changes in behaviour or academic performance.
- Displays signs of psychosis (hallucinations, delusions, disorganised thinking).
- Reports substance abuse as a coping mechanism.
- Presents with multiple converging risk factors (high stress + poor sleep + low activity + negative mood).
